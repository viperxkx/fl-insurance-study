●What Is Insurance?
▼

Insurance = the transfer of risk of loss from an individual to an insurance company, which spreads the cost among many people.

You pay a premium. The insurer is contractually obligated to pay claims as long as all terms are met.

Insurance transfers risk. Insurer spreads cost among all insureds.
●Law of Large Numbers
▼

The larger the pool of people with similar exposure, the more predictable actual losses become. This is how premiums are calculated.

Bigger pool = more predictable losses = fairer rates
●Insurable Interest
▼

You must stand to suffer a financial loss if the insured property is damaged. In P&C must exist at the time of the loss.

  • Financial (monetary stake)
  • Blood (family member)
  • Business (business partner)
●Risk — Pure vs. Speculative
▼
TypeOutcomeInsurable?
Pure RiskLoss only, or no change YES
Speculative RiskLoss OR gain (gambling) NO
ONLY pure risks are insurable!
●5 Elements of Insurable Risk
▼
  1. Due to chance — accidental, not intentional
  2. Definite & measurable — can be seen and quantified
  3. Statistically predictable — insurer can estimate frequency
  4. Not catastrophic — won't bankrupt the insurer
  5. Large loss exposure — enough people paying premiums
War is excluded — it is CATASTROPHIC; all could claim at once

●Peril vs. Hazard vs. Risk
▼
  • Risk = chance a loss WILL occur
  • Hazard = increases the PROBABILITY of loss
  • Peril = the actual CAUSE of the loss (fire, flood, theft)
Risk → Hazard increases it → Peril causes loss → Insurance covers it
️Indemnity & Subrogation
▼

Indemnity: Restores insured to same position as before loss. Collect only to extent of actual loss — no profiting from insurance.

Subrogation: After paying you, insurer can sue the at-fault party. Prevents collecting twice.

️Direct vs. Indirect Loss
▼
  • Direct: Physical damage to property itself
  • Indirect (Consequential): Economic loss FROM the direct loss (extra living expenses, lost rent, lost profits)

Fire damages home (direct). Water from hoses also covered — fire was the proximate cause.

Property insurance covers direct losses. Indirect requires added coverage.

️ Real Example: $1M per-occurrence / $2M aggregate

Feb: Ceiling collapse → $1M paid → $1M left in aggregate
Jul: Fire causes $1.5M loss → only $1M paid (all that remains)
After Jul: $0 aggregate — NO coverage until Jan 1, 2027

Once aggregate is exhausted, no coverage until policy anniversary
️Proximate Cause — Chain of Events
▼

The first negligent act that started an unbroken chain leading to injury/damage.

Driver runs red light → accident → utility pole down → power outage damages business. The red light violation is proximate cause of ALL damage.

Proximate cause = the first negligent act in the chain
●Coinsurance Formula
▼
(Insurance Carried ÷ Insurance Required) × Loss = Payment

Example: $100K building, 80% required = $80K needed. You carry $40K. Loss = $10K.
($40K ÷ $80K) × $10K = $5,000 paid. You absorb the other $5,000.

Total loss = coinsurance doesn't apply. Face amount paid in full.
●Underwriting, Rate & Loss Ratio
▼
  • Underwriting = risk selection at insurer's home office
  • Rate = cost per unit of insurance
  • Premium = actual dollar amount to keep policy in force
Loss Ratio = Losses Incurred ÷ Earned Premium

$75K claims ÷ $150K premium = 50% loss ratio

HIGH loss ratio = poor underwriting = insolvency risk
●FCRA, Certificate & Deposit Premium
▼
  • FCRA = administered by FTC. Protects consumers from inaccurate financial info.
  • Certificate of Insurance = proof a policy exists. Does NOT obligate insurer to holder.
  • Deposit Premium Audit = insurer can audit up to 3 years after expiration.
  • MedPay = no-fault coverage. No negligence proof needed.
●Ch. 2 Terms to Know
▼
TermDefinition
AppraisalA professional assessment of the value of property. In property claims, when insurer and insured can't agree on value, each selects an appraiser. If they can't agree, they pick an umpire.
ArbitrationCasualty-side equivalent of appraisal. Dispute is submitted to an arbitrator rather than each party selecting an appraiser.
Earned PremiumThe portion of a paid premium that belongs to the insurer because it covers the elapsed part of the policy period.
LiberalizationPolicy clause: if the insurer broadens coverage for new policies at no extra cost, existing policyholders automatically receive the same improved benefits immediately, without waiting for renewal.
SalvageThe amount recovered from the sale of damaged property after a total loss is paid. Insurer takes ownership of the damaged property and has the right to sell it.
PolicyownerThe owner of the insurance policy. May be different from the insured.
Third PartyA person who is not the insured or insurer but who may have rights under a policy (e.g., mortgagee, claimant).
UnderwritingProcess of evaluating and selecting risks for insurance coverage; determining whether to insure and at what premium.
️4 Elements of a Valid Contract — A.C.C.L.
▼
ElementIn Insurance
A — Agreement (Offer & Acceptance)Applicant submits application (offer). Insurer approves and issues policy (acceptance).
C — ConsiderationInsured: premium + statements on application. Insurer: promise to pay covered losses.
C — Competent PartiesBoth parties must be legally capable (of legal age, sound mind, not under duress).
L — Legal PurposeContract must not be for an illegal purpose. Insurance must have insurable interest.
→ Memory device: A.C.C.L. — "Always Check Contract Law"
●Representations, Warranties & Concealment
▼
TermDefinition & Key Exam Points
RepresentationStatement believed to be true to the best of one's knowledge — NOT guaranteed. Insured's application answers are representations. Material misrepresentation (changes underwriting decision) → voids policy. If intentional → fraud.
WarrantyAbsolutely true statement upon which the policy depends. Much stricter than a representation. Breach of warranty → can void policy or trigger return of premium.
ConcealmentIntentional withholding of material information that would affect the underwriting decision. May void the policy.
⏱️Binders
▼

A binder is a temporary agreement (written OR verbal) providing immediate coverage until a policy can be issued. The effective date = binder issue date. Expires when policy is issued or after a set number of days (varies by state). If policy is declined → binder expires the day after the insured receives notice of cancellation.

●Key Policy Provisions & Clauses
▼
ProvisionKey Rule
AssignmentTransfer of policy rights. ONLY valid with prior written consent of insurer.
AppraisalWhen insurer and insured disagree on value of a loss — either party makes written demand. Each picks appraiser; if no agreement, they pick an umpire.
Pro Rata Other InsuranceWhen multiple policies cover same risk, each pays proportional share based on limits. Insured CANNOT collect full loss from each policy.
Cancellation vs. NonrenewalCancellation = termination BEFORE expiration date (early). Nonrenewal = termination AT expiration (on time).
Standard Mortgage ClauseLoss paid to insured + mortgagee per their interest. Mortgagee's rights CANNOT be defeated by insured's acts. Insurer must give mortgagee advance written notice before cancellation. Mortgagee can pay premiums, sue in own name, submit proof of loss.
SalvageAfter total loss paid → insurer takes ownership of damaged property. Insurer can sell it to recoup part of the payout.
SubrogationAfter insurer pays insured, insured signs over right to collect from at-fault third party to insurer. Prevents double recovery. Based on indemnity principle.
Liberalization ClauseIf insurer improves coverage on new policies at no extra premium, existing policyholders get those benefits immediately.
→ Real property = non-movable (houses). Personal property = movable (autos, furniture, equipment).
●FCRA, Gramm-Leach-Bliley & TRIA
▼
LawKey Facts
FCRAFair Credit Reporting Act. Administered by FTC. Protects consumers from inaccurate or OBSOLETE personal/financial information. Consumer reports require legitimate business purpose.
Gramm-Leach-Bliley2 required disclosures: (1) when customer relationship established and (2) before disclosing protected information. Annual privacy notice required. Customer has right to opt out of sharing with nonaffiliated third parties.
TRIATerrorism Risk Insurance Act. Temporary federal program. Applies to commercial lines only. Certified by Treasury + Homeland Security + AG. 80% federal reimbursement above deductible (TRIPRA 2019, extended to Dec 31, 2027). $100B cap. Excludes: life/health, federal flood, medical malpractice, commercial auto, crop, reinsurance, burglary & theft, surety, professional liability (except D&O), farm owners.
●Dwelling Policy Forms (DP-1, DP-2, DP-3)
▼
FormTypeKey Detail
DP-1 (Basic)Named PerilMost limited. No minimum insurance required. Core perils: fire, lightning, internal explosion. Extended coverage perils added for extra premium: WHARVES (Windstorm, Hail, Aircraft/vehicles, Riot, Volcanic eruption, Explosion, Smoke).
DP-2 (Broad)Named PerilDP-1 + extended coverage + VMM + 7 additional broad perils (damage by burglars, falling objects, weight of ice/snow/sleet, accidental water discharge, electrical damage, sudden tearing/cracking, freezing — B.B. BICE-GOLF).
DP-3 (Special)Open Peril on A & B; Named Peril on CAll perils except specifically excluded. Dwelling and other structures = open peril. Personal property = broad named perils (same as DP-2).
→ Standard deductible: $250. Loss settlement: DP-1 = ACV. DP-2 & DP-3 = Replacement Cost if insured to at least 80% of replacement cost. Suit against insurer: within 2 years of loss.
→ VMM coverage excluded if premises vacant 60+ consecutive days.

●NFIP, Inland Marine & Other Policies
▼
TopicKey Facts
NFIPFederal flood program, administered by FEMA/FIMA. Flood = #1 natural disaster. 30-day waiting period. Single-family: $250K building / $100K contents. Policy term = 1 year; insured covered 30 days after expiration. WYO = private insurers write identical NFIP coverage under their own names.
Flood Definition2+ acres or 2+ properties inundated from: overflow of inland/tidal waters, rapid surface runoff, mudflow, coastal land collapse. Sewer backup, windblown rain = NOT a flood.
Inland MarineCovers movable property anywhere in the world. Personal Property Floater (all-risk, unscheduled), Personal Articles Floater (scheduled: jewelry, cameras, furs, fine arts), Personal Effects Floater (travelers' worn items, not at home).
EarthquakeExcluded from standard policies; added by endorsement. Multiple shocks within 72 hours = 1 occurrence. Deductible = % of loss.
Mobile HomesSame HO structure. Coverage C = 40% of Coverage A. Up to $500 to move to safety. Transportation/Permission to Move endorsement needed otherwise (adds collision, upset, stranding/sinking, 30 days, US or Canada).
●Personal Auto Policy (PAP) — Parts A–D
▼
PartCoverageKey Points
A — LiabilityBI & PD to others for which insured is legally liableSplit limits (BI/person / BI/accident / PD) or CSL. Defense costs paid IN ADDITION to limits. Exclusions: intentional, property owned by insured, public livery, unauthorized use, vehicles <4 wheels.
B — Medical PaymentsMedical & funeral expenses for insured injured in covered autoPer-person basis. Expenses within 3 years. No fault required.
C — UM/UIMBI damages from uninsured or underinsured at-fault driverUM = no insurance, less than minimum, hit-and-run, insolvent insurer. UIM formula: Insured UIM limit − at-fault BI limit = UIM payment. Hit-and-run = ALWAYS uninsured motorist.
D — Physical DamageDirect, accidental loss to covered or nonowned autoOnly if premium shown in Declarations. Collision = impact with vehicle/object. Other-than-collision (comprehensive) = fire, theft, vandalism, hail, flood, animals, glass. Transportation expense: 24-hr wait (48 for theft). Limit = lesser of ACV or repair/replace cost.
→ Policy territory: US, territories, possessions (Puerto Rico), and Canada.
→ Newly acquired auto: notify within 14 days for liability/med pay/UM. 14 days for physical damage if policy already has it; 4 days if not ($500 deductible if loss before notification).
️Umbrella & Excess Liability + PAP Parts E & F
▼
TermDefinition
Umbrella (stand-alone)Broader than underlying. Can cover losses underlying doesn't. Min $1M additional. If only umbrella covers loss → insured pays SIR (Self-Insured Retention).
Excess (follow-form)Mirrors underlying exactly — just increases the limit. No broader or narrower coverage.
SIRSelf-Insured Retention — deductible paid when only umbrella covers a loss (no underlying coverage).
Part E — Duties After LossPrompt notice to insurer. Cooperate. Forward legal papers. Submit to exam under oath. Allow insurer to inspect. Notify police for hit-and-run or theft.
Part F — General ProvisionsCancellation: 10 days for nonpayment/first 60 days; 20 days all other. Subrogation rights apply (not to physical damage by permitted driver). Assignment requires insurer's written consent.

️Unfair Trade Practices
▼
PracticeDefinition & Penalty
MisrepresentationFalse/misleading statements about policy terms, benefits, or insurer financial condition.
RebatingOffering anything of value not in the policy as inducement to buy. Both offer AND acceptance illegal. FL allows under strict schedule rules. Advertising gifts up to $100/year/person permitted.
TwistingMisleading comparisons to induce switching to a different insurer. 1st-degree misdemeanor. Fine: $12,500/nonwillful; $187,500/willful.
ChurningCanceling and rewriting within the SAME insurer to earn extra commissions. Same penalties as twisting.
SlidingTelling applicant a secondary coverage is required by law when it isn't, or adding charges without consent.
DefamationFalse oral or written statements intended to injure a person/company in insurance.
Controlled BusinessWriting insurance on own life/property or family/associates. Legal only if commissions ≤50% of all commissions in 12 months.
→ FIGA covers P&C claims of insolvent insurers up to $300,000/claim (after first $100). Additional $200K for homeowners structure/contents. Does NOT cover: life, health, annuities, workers comp, ocean marine, title, surplus lines.
→ Cease and desist violation: fine up to $50,000 + license action. Forging signatures = 3rd degree felony. Transacting without license = 3rd degree felony.

Chapter 2 — Video Lesson Transcripts
▶Video: Insureds
▼
Ch. 2 — Policy Provisions 2:15

Different people may be insured under a property and liability policy — not everyone is specifically identified. That's why we have insured designations.

Named Insured — the individual whose name appears on the policy's declarations page. Generally, whoever purchases the policy (e.g., the homeowner).

First Named Insured — the individual whose name appears first on the declarations. There may be several named insureds, but only one first named insured. Has duties including: paying the premium and giving prompt notice of a claim. Especially important on commercial policies — this is the insurer's primary contact with the business.

Additional Insureds — individuals or businesses not named on the declarations page, but protected by the policy for a specific financial interest. Added by endorsement. Examples: mortgage company, lienholder on a vehicle.

If a house burns down, the mortgageholder may be entitled to part of the loss payment. If a vehicle is totaled with an outstanding loan, the insurance company pays off the lender first. This ensures the correct parties get paid when a loss occurs.

→ Key exam point: Additional insureds are added by endorsement. They have a specific financial interest — like a lender or lienholder.
▶Video: Binders and Subrogation
▼
Ch. 2 — Policy Provisions 1:43

Binders — A binder is a temporary agreement (written or oral) providing coverage until a policy can be issued. It gives the insured immediate temporary coverage. Binders expire once the policy is issued or after a set number of days (varies by state). The effective date equals the date the binder was issued. If the policy is declined, the binder expires the day after receipt of the cancellation notice.

Subrogation — The legal right of the insurance company to seek damages from a third party after it has reimbursed the insured for a loss.

Example: You're in a hit-and-run accident. Your insurer pays your claim and repairs your vehicle. When you accept the claim check, you transfer your right to seek reimbursement from the other driver to the insurance company. The insurer can then sue the at-fault party to recover the amount paid.

→ Subrogation: insured gets paid first → insured signs over their right to collect from the third party → insurer pursues the third party. Prevents double recovery.
▶Video: Policy Structure
▼
Ch. 2 — Policy Provisions 3:09

The Insurance Services Office (ISO) creates standardized P&C insurance forms adopted by individual states. This is why policies from two different companies look very similar.

Declarations ("dec page") — First section. Who is the insurer? Who is the insured? What property is covered? What are the limits? What is the premium?

Definitions — Clarifies terms. Bold, italic, or quoted words are defined here.

Insuring Agreement — Spells out the insurer's obligation to provide coverage. Lists parties, effective/renewal dates, coverage description, and perils. The insured promises to pay premium; the insurer promises to pay covered claims.

Additional/Supplementary Coverages — Extra coverage for specific loss expenses, usually at no additional premium.

Conditions — Rules both parties follow. Includes: insurer's right to inspect, how policy may be changed, refund processing if cancelled early.

Endorsements — Written addendums changing the policy. Must be in writing, attached to policy, signed by executive officer. Used to add/delete coverage or correct info.

Exclusions — What is NOT covered. Can exclude people, property, or perils.

Exam Acronym from Video

Declarations  ·  Insuring Agreement  ·  Conditions  ·  Exclusions

DICE — the major components of any P&C policy

▶Video: Common Policy Provisions
▼
Ch. 2 — Policy Provisions 4:17

Appraisal vs. Arbitration — Both mean the same thing: when the insurer and insured can't agree on a claim, a written request brings in a third party to settle the dispute. In property claims each party selects an appraiser. In casualty claims it goes to an arbitrator.

Assignment — Transfer of rights or interest in a policy to someone else. Only valid with prior written consent of the insurer.

Other Insurance — When an insured has multiple policies on the same property, losses are shared proportionally among the insurers.

Notice of Claim — When the insured calls and reports a loss. The insurer must acknowledge receipt, provide forms/instructions/assistance, and begin investigation.

Proof of Loss — When the insured completes, signs, and submits the claim form. This is the sworn proof of loss.

Liberalization Clause — If the insurer improves coverage on new policies at no extra premium, ALL current policyholders get those same improved benefits immediately — no waiting for renewal.

Subrogation — When the insured accepts a claim payment, they waive their right to seek damages from the at-fault third party and give that right to the insurer. Based on the principle of indemnity — prevents the insured from collecting twice.

Salvage — The amount recovered from the sale of damaged property after a total loss is paid. The insurer abandons interest to the insured and takes the right to salvage. Example: after paying a totaled car claim, the insurer takes the car and sells it for parts to recoup money.

Third Party Provisions — Protect interests of parties like mortgagees. A third party's right to recover is limited to their insurable interest. Third parties must receive written advance notice if a policy is cancelled or nonrenewed.

→ Appraisal = property disputes. Arbitration = casualty disputes. Same concept, different sides.
→ Salvage: total loss paid → insurer takes ownership of the damaged property and sells it. That's how they recoup part of the payout.
